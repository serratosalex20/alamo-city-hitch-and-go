Navbar Comparison

Direction A (glued glass) vs Direction B (floating pill). Mobile + desktop side by side. Real brand tokens, live logo URL.

Direction A

Glued Glass-on-Ink

Standard sticky navbar pinned to the very top edge. Ink background at 70% opacity with a strong backdrop-blur — the classic "floating glass" effect you see on Linear, Stripe, Resend, and most premium SaaS sites in 2026. Hairline bottom border defines the edge. Logo-only on mobile, logo + wordmark on desktop. Safe, polished, ages well.

Pros

  • Familiar pattern — visitors don't have to learn anything new
  • Maximum visible content area (no extra margin around the bar)
  • Easier to nail responsive behavior across edge cases
  • Reads as polished and grown-up; same family as Stripe / Linear / Resend
  • ~30 min to implement, low responsive-QA risk

Cons

  • Less brand-distinctive than a floating pill
  • Doesn't catch the eye on its own — relies on logo + hero to do the work
  • Visually similar to most SaaS marketing sites (good or bad, depending)
Direction B

Floating Pill

Same glass treatment but the entire header is a rounded-full pill that floats away from the top edge with 12–16px of margin. Sits over the page content rather than glued to it. This is the 2026 Awwwards/Dribbble pattern of choice — Vercel 2025 redesign, Granola, recent Framer templates. Higher visual ceiling, more brand-distinctive, but takes longer to nail edge cases (scrollbar offset, small viewports, when content scrolls underneath).

Pros

  • Distinctive — feels designed, not templated
  • Floating pill is the strongest 2026 navbar trend (Vercel, Granola, Framer)
  • Better visual hierarchy: the pill says "this is the brand" before content even loads
  • Ambient drop shadow under the pill adds depth without being heavy
  • Sets a higher design ceiling for the rest of the site to match

Cons

  • ~2× the responsive-QA work (very-narrow phones, ultrawide, scrollbar offset)
  • The pill silhouette can feel cramped if the nav link count grows
  • Some users perceive floating UI as "still loading" for a second on slow connections
  • Backdrop-blur is expensive — slight perf hit on older Android

My recommendation: A now, B later

Ship Direction A as part of the current sprint — it's a clean, durable upgrade over the current 4-line-wrap navbar and lets us close the navbar issue this hour. Once you're driving real traffic and have customer feedback, do Direction B as part of a "v2 polish" pass when the design ceiling for the rest of the site has caught up. Going straight to B now means we're polishing one component to a higher level than the surrounding pages have reached yet — risky.
